# ai201-project4-provenance-guard
---
## Project Description
AI Content Provenance Detector is a backend system that classifies submitted creative text as likely AI-generated, likely human-written, or uncertain, and lets creators appeal incorrect classifications.

It addresses the trust problem in online creative spaces where readers can’t reliably tell whether content was written by a human or generated by AI. Instead of giving a single “AI vs human” label, the system focuses on transparency and confidence.

A single detector can easily be wrong, flagging polished human writing as AI or missing AI-generated text entirely. This system handles that by showing how confident it is, surfacing that to users, and allowing creators to challenge decisions.

Provenance Guard runs two detection signals, combines them into a calibrated confidence score, generates a simple reader-facing label, and logs all decisions and appeals for accountability.
---
## Architecture Overview

A submission (`POST /submit`) is processed by two independent detection signals: an LLM-based judgment (Groq, llama-3.3-70b-versatile) and a stylometric heuristic. Each produces a 0–1 score, which are combined into a single confidence score using a weighted average.

That confidence score is mapped deterministically to one of three transparency labels. The full result — both signal scores, combined confidence, final label, and attribution — is stored in a structured audit log and returned in the response.

Creators can contest a decision via `POST /appeal`, which attaches their reasoning to the existing log entry and changes its status to `under_review`. The appeal does not re-run detection; it only records a dispute for later review.

```
POST /submit {text, creator_id}
↓
Signal 1: Groq LLM judgment (0-1)
↓
Signal 2: Stylometric heuristics (0-1)
↓
Confidence Scoring (0.7llm + 0.3stylometry)
↓
Label Generation (score → label text)
↓
Audit Log
↓
Response {content_id, attribution, confidence, label}
POST /appeal {content_id, creator_reasoning}
↓
Update status → "under_review"
↓
Audit Log (appends reasoning)
↓
Response {status, confirmation}
```

## Detection Signals

**Signal 1 — LLM judgment (Groq, llama-3.3-70b-versatile).**  
The text is sent to Groq with a prompt asking it to estimate a 0–1 probability that the content is AI-generated. This signal picks up semantic flow, tone, and overall coherence — basically whether the writing *reads* like a human voice.

It works well on nuance and context, but it’s not verifying authorship. It’s still just pattern-based judgment. Emotional or story-like AI text can easily look human, and very structured human writing (academic, formal, technical) can get pushed toward AI. It’s sensitive to “how it feels”, not what it is.

**Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python).**  
This looks only at structure: average sentence length and sentence-length variance, normalized into a 0–1 score.

The assumption is that AI writing tends to be more uniform, while human writing is more irregular. In practice, this breaks in predictable ways — minimalist writing, academic writing, ESL writing, or children’s text can all look “too uniform” and get flagged as AI even when they’re clearly human. It never looks at meaning, only form.

**Combining them.**  
`confidence = 0.7 * llm_score + 0.3 * stylometry_score`

LLM is weighted higher because it performs better on real-world text and captures semantic signals stylometry misses. Stylometry is still included as a backup signal for structural patterns that the LLM can overlook, especially in edge cases where tone is misleading.

Together, they form a two-view system: one looks at meaning and coherence, the other at structure and consistency.

## Confidence Scoring

Both signals output a 0–1 score, combined via a weighted average: 
`confidence = 0.7 * llm_score + 0.3 * stylometry_score`. LLM is weighted higher 
because in testing it was consistently the stronger, more discriminating signal; 
stylometry is kept in the mix specifically to catch cases where LLM judgment is 
fooled by emotional tone (see Known Limitations).

**Thresholds:**

| Score range | Label |
|---|---|
| 0.00 – 0.35 | Likely human |
| 0.35 – 0.70 | Uncertain |
| 0.70 – 1.00 | Likely AI |

These thresholds are intentionally skewed — a false positive (calling a human's 
work AI) is more damaging to a creator than a false negative, so the system 
requires stronger evidence before landing in "likely AI."

**Validation — two example submissions with different confidence:**

- **High-confidence case:** A clearly AI-style paragraph about artificial 
  intelligence and stakeholder collaboration scored `llm_score: 0.80`, 
  `stylometry_score: 0.39`, combined `confidence: 0.675` → labeled Uncertain 
  (bordering on Likely AI).
- **Low-confidence case:** A casual first-person review of a ramen restaurant 
  scored `llm_score: 0.20`, `stylometry_score: 0.28`, combined 
  `confidence: 0.224` → labeled Likely human.

The clear separation between these two scores (0.224 vs. 0.675) shows the scoring 
produces meaningful variation rather than a constant or near-binary output.

---
## Transparency Label

| Variant | Text shown to reader |
|---|---|
| **Likely AI** (score 0.70–1.00) | "This content is likely AI-generated. Our system found strong signs of AI authorship, but no detector is 100% accurate." |
| **Uncertain** (score 0.35–0.70) | "We're not confident whether this content was written by a human or AI. Read it with that uncertainty in mind." |
| **Likely Human** (score 0.00–0.35) | "This content shows strong signs of human authorship." |

Each label is written in plain language with no technical jargon (no "classifier 
output," "logit," or raw score shown as the primary message). The "Likely AI" 
label specifically avoids sounding like a verdict — it explicitly names the 
detector's imperfection, since a false accusation is the worst-case outcome for 
a creator.

## Rate Limiting

Applied via Flask-Limiter on `POST /submit`: **10 requests per minute, 100 per day**.

Reasoning: a single creator submitting their own work — even iterating on drafts — 
realistically submits a handful of times per session, not dozens per minute. 10/min 
comfortably covers that while blocking a script attempting to flood the endpoint. 
100/day allows a legitimate, active user to test many pieces across a day without 
hitting a wall, while still bounding sustained abuse.

**Evidence — 12 rapid requests sent to `/submit`:**
```
200
200
200
200
200
200
200
200
200
200
429
429
```
The first 10 succeeded; the 11th and 12th were rejected with `429 Too Many Requests`, 
confirming the limit triggers correctly.

## Known Limitations

**Formal / academic human writing can get flagged as AI.** In testing, a human-written excerpt on monetary policy and central bank tradeoffs scored `llm_score: 0.70`, `stylometry_score: 0.77`, resulting in `confidence: 0.72` → **Likely AI**.

This happens because both signals independently reward similar surface traits: consistent sentence length, formal vocabulary, and low structural variance. The LLM leans on tone/register (formal writing gets read as AI-like), while stylometry leans on structure (low variance = AI-like). So instead of balancing each other, they end up reinforcing the same bias. As a result, academic writing, ESL writing, or any intentionally structured human writing is more likely to get false positives.

**Narrative fiction is relatively stable across edits.** I tested three versions of the same short scene (original, lightly edited, heavily polished), and all stayed in a tight range (`confidence: 0.278, 0.243, 0.29`) — all **Likely human**.

This suggests the LLM signal is more sensitive to *genre and content type* than surface polish. Narrative, sensory writing tends to read as human almost by default, even when sentence structure becomes more uniform. Since stylometry only contributes 30% of the final score, it’s not strong enough to shift the result much on its own.

In practice, this makes the system more sensitive to formal / argumentative writing than fiction. A polished AI-generated story could potentially slip through as human, especially if it mimics narrative flow well enough.

## Spec Reflection

**How the spec helped:** writing `planning.md` early (especially the "Anticipated Edge Cases" section) helped a lot during testing. I had already predicted that very polished, formal human writing could get flagged as AI because of low structural variance. That exact case showed up in Milestone 4 — a real monetary-policy excerpt scored `confidence: 0.72` and landed in **Likely AI**.

Because I had already documented that risk, it didn’t feel like a bug or surprise. It was immediately recognizable as an expected failure mode. It also made it easier to explain clearly in the README instead of trying to justify it after the fact.

**Where implementation diverged:** the spec originally used `confidence = 0.6 * llm_score + 0.4 * stylometry_score`. After testing both signals across the required inputs (clearly AI, clearly human, and borderline cases), the LLM signal consistently matched intuition better, while stylometry stayed relatively flat and less sensitive on its own.

So I changed the weighting to `0.7 / 0.3` to reflect that gap. LLM carries more of the decision since it captures meaning and tone, while stylometry is still kept because it helps in cases where structure matters (like formal writing), even though it can also contribute to false positives.

## AI Usage

**Instance 1 — Stylometric signal function.** I used Claude to generate the initial stylometry function based on my spec (sentence-length variance, type-token ratio, punctuation density combined into one score). On paper it matched the plan, but when I tested it on the four benchmark inputs, the results didn’t make sense — the “clearly AI” text ended up scoring the lowest, and all four outputs were basically compressed into a tight range (around 0.18–0.28), which made the signal useless.

Instead of trying to endlessly tune all three metrics, I simplified the approach. I dropped type-token ratio and punctuation density and kept just average sentence length + sentence-length variance. I rewrote the scoring logic manually, and that immediately fixed the spread — the scores became more separated and actually aligned with the expected ordering across the test cases.

**Instance 2 — Audit log update helper for appeals.** I used Claude to implement an `update_log_entry()` helper in `audit_log.py` that finds an entry by `content_id` and merges in new fields like status, appeal reasoning, and timestamp without modifying the rest of the log entry.

The first version had a silent failure issue — if the `content_id` didn’t exist, it would just return successfully without updating anything. That meant a bad or typo’d appeal request could still return a “success” even though nothing actually changed.

I caught this during testing and fixed it by making the function return a boolean indicating whether a match was found. I then wired that into the `/appeal` route so invalid `content_id`s correctly return a `404` instead of a false success response.
